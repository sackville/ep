# Viper Command Through the Agile Development Lens

*Preserved source analysis for EP process reconciliation. This is a situated assessment of Viper Command, not reconciled EP guidance.*

## Governing concern

From an agile development perspective, Command has completed much of its initial product discovery and framing but has not yet entered empirical delivery. It has designed intent but no working increment producing feedback.

## Work completed or substantially underway

### Product vision and goal

Command has a clear direction: maintain shared understanding, reveal the path toward objectives, and answer what to do next.

### Real primary user and problem

The product is anchored to Scott's real work, current tools, friction, emotional stakes, desired outcomes, and immediate feedback access.

### Bounded MVP

The project has explicitly deferred real-time collaboration, recurrence, bets, soft edges, achievements, full altitude behavior, and view reorientation. Single-user screen sharing is sufficient initially.

### Experience journey

The six beats resemble an early story map: establish the world, orient, cascade, align for a meeting, absorb change, and define unknown process.

### Product and interaction constraints

The design principles constrain backlog choices: preserve shared understanding, reveal consequence and uncertainty, keep the board truthful, reward understanding, and reduce reorientation cost.

### Early prototypes

The SVG scenes are design probes that make the proposed vocabulary inspectable before implementation.

### Product hypotheses and feedback signals

The persona records behavioral signals such as repeated voluntary use, meeting use, trust, requests for new capability, and reaching for Command before a whiteboard. It also records failure signals.

## Work not yet completed

- Ordered product backlog.
- Thin, independently testable user stories.
- Acceptance criteria.
- Explicit first release goal.
- Definition of Done.
- Delivery cadence.
- Working application increment.
- Feedback and instrumentation loop.
- Evidence from real use.

The story beats are good source material, but each remains too broad to be one delivery story.

## Recommended next work

### 1. State the first release goal

Describe the smallest useful dogfood outcome. A provisional goal is that Scott can open a real formation board, understand its state, identify available work, complete a Task or settle a Decision, and see what that progress changes.

### 2. Build an initial story map

Organize work around activities such as open and orient, explore, inspect, advance, record, see consequences, correct the model, and prepare for a meeting. Draw the first usable release boundary across them.

### 3. Slice stories vertically

Prefer user-visible slices over component work. Examples:

- Scott can see one real Objective with completed, Available, and Locked work and identify what is actionable.
- Completing an Available Task preserves its result and makes one downstream Task Available.
- Settling a Decision preserves its rationale and changes the board.

### 4. Add observable acceptance criteria

Criteria should express what Scott can understand or accomplish, such as identifying current work, ownership, and blockers without opening another view.

### 5. Order by learning and risk

Prioritize uncertainties: board legibility, rapid orientation, cascade comprehension, maintenance burden, comparison with a whiteboard, and whether playability helps rather than distracts.

### 6. Establish a lightweight Definition of Done

A completed story should pass its criteria with representative data, preserve relevant history, include appropriate automated coverage, run in the dogfood environment, survive user review in the application, and update shared-memory documentation where needed.

### 7. Build the tracer bullet

Start the application, load a tiny real board, render Wake and Frontier states, settle or complete one item, preserve it, and show a downstream consequence. Use real persistence and real interaction even if they are crude.

### 8. Dogfood immediately

Use it for actual formation work. Observe disagreements with reality, missing context, maintenance burden, whether the cascade clarifies, and when Scott abandons Command for another tool.

### 9. Review and reorder after each usable increment

Demonstrate with real data, compare against the product goal, record learning, add or remove work, reorder the backlog, and choose the next smallest valuable experiment.

## Lens summary

Command is well prepared for development: its vision, user, scope, journey, constraints, and hypotheses are clear. The next agile transition is to slice narrowly, build the tracer bullet, use it on real work, learn, and reorder.
