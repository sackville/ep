# ISS and Viper Command Through the Lean Startup and BMC Lens

*Preserved source analysis for EP process reconciliation. This is a situated assessment, not reconciled EP guidance.*

## Governing concern

ISS has done substantial vision and hypothesis formation but comparatively little validated learning. The company has strong reasons for existing and several promising beliefs; its next job is to make those beliefs explicit and falsifiable.

The Business Model Canvas is not part of Eric Ries's Lean Startup framework, but the two are complementary: the BMC inventories business-model hypotheses, while Lean Startup supplies the experimental loop for testing them. Customer Development supplies much of the fieldwork for customer, problem, channel, and purchasing assumptions.

Executable Standards is the likely first market product. Command is currently an internal product and probe; its marketability should remain an option until dogfooding produces evidence worth pursuing.

## Work completed or substantially underway

### Durable company purpose

ISS exists to protect lives, health, time, and resources from preventable failure by building the shared understanding needed for well-informed decisions. This can remain stable while products and business models change.

### Complementary founding capabilities

The founders contribute system safety, model-based systems engineering, enterprise software, and architecture. Founding-team compatibility remains an important startup hypothesis, not a concern separate from the business.

### Explicit leap-of-faith hypotheses

Three beliefs are already named:

1. Executable Standards are technically sound and commercially valuable.
2. Playable interfaces can improve enterprise software.
3. The Viper Method can grow a platform through demand-pull evolution.

### Technical proof and early contact

Executable Standards has a working proof of concept. Presentations to regulated-industry engineers provide early evidence of interest, though not yet necessarily adoption or willingness to pay.

### Real internal problem and Command value hypothesis

The founders experience scattered context, diverging mental models, invisible work, lost rationale, and repeated reconstruction. Command hypothesizes that a living, playable representation can improve shared understanding and next-action confidence.

### Constrained MVP and early signals

Command avoids many expensive assumptions and defines behavioral evidence such as voluntary repeated use, meeting use, trust, requests, and preference over a whiteboard.

## Important beliefs still unvalidated

- Russ and Cynthia experience the problem as Scott does.
- They will engage with Command rather than regard it as Scott's private system.
- The warm tech tree improves understanding.
- Board maintenance costs less than the confusion prevented.
- Command meaningfully outperforms or complements a whiteboard.
- Internal value generalizes to an external market.
- Customers will adopt and pay for a Command-like product.
- Executable Standards has repeatable commercial demand.
- ISS's prospective products form a coherent company strategy.
- The founders can repeatedly work through disagreement.
- Demand-pull platform growth works under real pressure.

## Recommended Lean Startup work

### 1. Separate the company's experiments

Keep distinct hypotheses for Executable Standards, Viper Command, Enterprise Playability, Viper Method, and founding-team behavior. Evidence for one must not be mistaken for validation of the whole company.

### 2. Map and rank assumptions

Inventory assumptions about problem severity, alternatives, users, buyers, adoption, value, technical feasibility, maintenance, channels, revenue, and founding behavior. Rank them by damage if false and weakness of current evidence.

### 3. Establish a baseline

Observe how formation currently works: reorientation time, repeated conversations, unclear ownership, information sources, manual synchronization, and mental-model divergence.

### 4. Test the problem with other founders

Ask Russ and Cynthia about recent concrete episodes and observe actual behavior rather than asking whether they like the proposed solution.

### 5. Run the cheapest credible Command experiment

Use a manually maintained board or facilitated prototype with real formation data before building the full application. Test whether it reduces status reconstruction, clarifies availability, supports decisions and Tasks together, communicates ownership, and is requested again.

### 6. Set success and failure thresholds in advance

Possible criteria include repeated use in real sessions, use by a founder other than Scott to answer a question, faster identification of work, reasonable maintenance cost, and discovery of discrepancies in shared understanding.

### 7. Build only what the next learning cycle requires

Automate the smallest loop that lets the team load a real board, orient, record progress, see consequences, preserve history, and return later.

### 8. Measure learning rather than vanity

Track voluntary use, reorientation time, meetings using the board, later consultation of records, updates caused by new information, unowned work discovered, repeated conversations avoided, founder-originated requests, and maintenance effort.

### 9. Hold explicit persevere/adapt/pivot reviews

Record the hypothesis, expectation, result, surprise, changed belief, and next riskiest assumption. Treat internal-tool status, narrowed problem, changed interaction, changed user, or reprioritized ISS product as legitimate outcomes.

### 10. Begin external discovery before generalizing

If Command succeeds internally, investigate whether others experience the same costly problem, who owns it and its budget, what creates urgency, and what prevents adoption.

## Integrating Business Model Canvas hypotheses

### Start now and label uncertainty honestly

Create a dated **Executable Standards Business Model Canvas — Hypothesis Draft**, not a plan presented as fact. Blank boxes and explicit unknowns are preferable to invented certainty.

Keep product-specific canvases separate. Do not blend Executable Standards, Command, DSO, and NMO into one canvas; their users, buyers, value, channels, revenue, costs, and barriers may differ.

### Convert each canvas statement into a testable record

Each hypothesis should capture:

```text
Hypothesis
Type
Importance
Current evidence
Confidence
Test
Expected result
Failure threshold
Result
Decision
```

### Customer Segments

Distinguish users, beneficiaries, champions, economic buyers, approvers, and blockers. For Executable Standards, investigate standards owners, practicing engineers, assurance personnel, engineering leaders, regulators, tool owners, and executives who bear failure costs.

### Value Propositions

Investigate outcomes rather than novelty: reduced interpretation inconsistency, earlier nonconformance detection, shorter assurance cycles, better traceability, less rework, easier change propagation, preserved knowledge, and fewer failures.

### Channels

Treat founder outreach, professional networks, industry presentations, standards bodies, conferences, tool partners, integrators, and paid proofs of concept as hypotheses. Record whether interest advances to concrete action.

### Customer Relationships

Test how much collaborative discovery, encoding, integration, training, validation, and continued support adoption requires, and whether customers can eventually author and maintain standards independently.

### Revenue Streams

Test paid discovery, proofs of concept, services, authoring fees, subscription, enterprise licensing, usage pricing, support, and licensing through standards owners. Strong evidence includes funded pilots, identified budgets, and procurement movement—not stated willingness alone.

### Key Resources

Investigate whether the scarce resources are intellectual property, domain expertise, modeling and software capability, access to representative systems, customer trust, tooling, or content rights.

### Key Activities

Investigate discovery, encoding, validation, workflow integration, demonstration, adoption support, maintenance, and demand-pulled platform development. Determine whether delivery can become repeatable without unlimited founder effort.

### Key Partnerships

Possible partners include standards organizations, regulators, tool vendors, consultancies, integrators, research organizations, design partners, and IP specialists. Specify the concrete contribution: access, credibility, distribution, rights, expertise, integration, or acceptance.

### Cost Structure

Estimate founder time, encoding, discovery and sales, integration, validation, software, cloud, legal/IP, support, and ongoing maintenance. Early unit economics should ask how much expert effort produces one unit of customer value.

### Prioritize fatal assumptions

Do not test all boxes evenly. Start with whether a specific customer has a costly problem, values the proposed outcome enough to act, has a reachable buyer, will commit time/data/money, can receive the result from ISS, can be served economically, and can be reached repeatedly.

## Integrated Lean/BMC sequence

1. Keep purpose stable.
2. Separate product and company hypotheses.
3. Draft an Executable Standards BMC.
4. Convert its contents into ranked hypotheses.
5. Record evidence and confidence.
6. Select the riskiest assumption.
7. Design the cheapest credible test.
8. Set thresholds before running it.
9. Run the experiment.
10. Separate evidence from interpretation.
11. Update the canvas and hypothesis register.
12. Persevere, modify the business model, or pivot.

## Lens summary

The BMC states what ISS currently believes about how Executable Standards could become a viable business. Lean Startup supplies the method for discovering which beliefs survive contact with reality. For Command, the next transition is from compelling product intent to explicit assumptions, baseline observation, cheap experiments, behavioral evidence, and deliberate learning.
