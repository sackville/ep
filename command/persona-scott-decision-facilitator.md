# Primary Persona: Scott

*Version 2. A living document.*
*Anchored to a single real person at full fidelity — the dogfooding persona for the first ISS application.*
*Two audiences: the AI collaborator who needs this loaded before touching the design, and the founding team, who need to understand why the application exists at all.*

---

## Why a persona at all

The method is one real person in a situation, carrying their real jobs — not a role abstraction, not an average of several people. The persona keeps the jobs situated; the jobs keep the persona honest.

This one is unusual in a useful way: the person is simultaneously the user, the builder, and the design collaborator. That creates the shortest possible feedback loop. It also creates a risk. When the user, builder, and collaborator are all the same person, the tension between "what the user needs" and "what is easy or interesting to build" can quietly disappear. Capturing the persona externalizes it and makes that drift visible.

Informally partnered with:

## The situation

### The business

Three major product lines exist at different stages of maturity:

- **Executable Standards** — Russ's invention and primary commercialization target. A working proof-of-concept already exists.
- **Dynamic System Observatory (DSO)** — a live simulation and digital-twin environment.
- **Near-Miss Observatory (NMO)** — an extension of DSO focused on identifying incident precursors before they reach production.

The team's strongest traction to date comes from the executable-standards proof-of-concept and recent presentations to regulated-industry engineers.

### Scott's responsibilities

Scott is responsible for:

- guiding company formation,
- facilitating strategic decisions,
- architecting the Viper Platform,
- building the team's internal applications,
- coordinating with counsel,
- and acting as the integration layer between information sources.

Much of the formation process currently lives across:

- email,
- meeting notes,
- Word documents,
- attorney correspondence,
- whiteboards,
- and memory.

Scott serves as the human system that keeps those sources synchronized.

### The challenge

The primary challenge is not lack of information.

The challenge is maintaining shared understanding as information changes.

Each founder maintains a different mental model of the company, its priorities, its opportunities, and its next steps. Those models often converge during discussion and then gradually diverge again afterward as memory fades and new information arrives.

The team spends significant time reconciling those mental models.

Scott does not view this diversity of perspective as a problem. In many cases it is a strength. Different perspectives produce better decisions.

The problem is that the convergence is temporary.

The shared understanding rarely has a durable home.

### The emotional stakes

The greatest concern is not filing paperwork incorrectly.

It is forming a company with the wrong partnership dynamics.

Differences in communication style between co-founders occasionally create friction, slow progress, and increase uncertainty about the long-term viability of the founding team.

This uncertainty affects decisions about formation itself and contributes to hesitation around advancing the process.

The application's jobs decompose a deeper job:

> Maintain sufficient shared understanding for the team to confidently decide what to do next.

## Jobs to be done

### Out of scope

Scott's broader responsibilities include:

- Architecting the Viper Platform.
- Building internal applications.
- Filing the corporation.
- Coordinating patent work.
- Supporting commercialization efforts.

These remain real jobs but are not the focus of this application.

### In scope

The application serves five MVP jobs.

#### 1. Maintain one durable, current, shared picture of reality.

The board becomes the persistent memory of the team.

#### 2. Understand what a completed decision changes.

Progress becomes visible through consequences.

#### 3. Answer "What's next?"

The application should help determine the most valuable next step by making visible:

- urgency,
- dependencies,
- blockers,
- effort,
- divisibility,
- and impact.

#### 4. Keep the picture current as reality changes.

New information must be incorporated quickly and easily.

The map should assume it is correct while remaining easy to correct.

#### 5. Define or select the process governing a node.

The system distinguishes between:

- process-defined work,
- and process-undefined work.

Undefined work is not a failure state. It is an honest representation of reality.

## Failure modes

The current approach fails in several recurring ways.

### Partial context

Important information exists but is not visible when needed.

This leads to decisions or statements being made from incomplete understanding.

### Scattered memory

Decision records, rationale, and context become fragmented across communication channels.

### Dependency blindness

Effort estimates are often wrong because downstream dependencies are not fully understood.

### Diverging mental models

Participants leave a meeting aligned and gradually drift apart afterward.

### Repeated conversations

Questions previously settled return because the shared understanding was never durably captured.

---

## Shared understanding

Shared understanding is not achieved when people agree temporarily.

Shared understanding is achieved when:

1. The understanding can be restated in plain language.
2. It is captured in a durable form.
3. It can be explained to someone who was not present.
4. The explanation survives feedback and review.

For simpler decisions, capturing the options considered and the option selected may be sufficient.

For more complex decisions, rationale and explanatory context become necessary.

The board exists to provide the persistent baseline from which mental models can repeatedly reconverge.

## Scenarios

### 1. Orient and pick up the thread

After several days away from formation work, Scott opens the board and immediately understands:

- where the team stands,
- what changed,
- and what comes next.

### 2. Watch a decision cascade

A decision is settled.

The resulting consequences become visible immediately.

The reward is increased understanding.

### 3. Walk into a meeting already aligned

The board provides a shared baseline so the meeting can focus on decisions rather than status reconstruction.

### 4. Absorb new information

Counsel introduces a new requirement.

The map changes quickly and remains truthful.

### 5. Encounter process-undefined work

The application refuses to invent a path forward.

Instead it honestly reveals that no governing process exists and directs attention toward defining one.

## Evidence the experiment is working

The earliest indicators of success are behavioral.

1. The team chooses to use Command repeatedly.
2. Requests emerge for additional capabilities.
3. The application gets used in ways not originally anticipated.
4. The team begins viewing it as a source of truth.
5. Customers are identified who might benefit from similar capabilities.

The strongest signal of all:

> The team reaches for Command before reaching for a whiteboard.

---

## Evidence the experiment is failing

The earliest warning sign is silence.

Additional signals include:

- only Scott uses the application,
- the board is ignored during meetings,
- participants prefer spreadsheets,
- participants prefer whiteboards,
- the board is not trusted,
- or the playable elements make understanding harder rather than easier.

Failure to outperform a whiteboard is not necessarily failure of the experiment.

A whiteboard is a strong competitor.

The test is whether the software eventually earns advantages that justify its existence.
